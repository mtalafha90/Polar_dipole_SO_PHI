Result 1 · Coverage

PHI's polar advantage switches on — and off — with SolO's latitude

≥60° polar-cap fill fraction, PHI vs HMI, per Carrington rotation. Coverage is a count of observed bins — independent of calibration and of any polar-fill assumption, so this is the most robust result. As SolO dives to −17° it wins the south cap; as it climbs to +17° it wins the north by an order of magnitude (peaking at 77% vs 12% across the full CR 2297 apex rotation); as it descends again the advantage fades symmetrically, with HMI recovering as Earth's own B₀ rises into northern summer.

Fills use the conservative limb cut µmin = 0.4 (only pixels within ~66° of disk centre, where the radial field is reliable). The advantage's sign is robust to the cut; its size is not — at a permissive µmin = 0.25 the April north gap narrows to 86% vs 33% (~2.6×) as HMI admits grazing-angle polar pixels. The 16× figure is a statement about reliable coverage.

Why the north matters and the south doesn't

Constraining the north cap, where HMI is effectively blind, injects a strong polar field where the Earth view has none — flipping the initial dipole sign (g₁₀(0) = −0.26 vs +0.10 G) and pulling the first predicted reversal 1.68 yr earlier. Constraining the co-observed south cap changes nothing, because HMI already constrains that pole. The 22-yr dipoles converge (~+3.1 G) as the source dominates: the memory lives in reversal timing, not the asymptote.

Absolute g₁₀ is provisional; coverage and the SFT differential are not

The partial-coverage products don't reproduce this near-zero signal in sign (all +0.3 to +1.0 G). Unlike a dense window, a near-zero true dipole sampled from partial per-rotation longitude coverage is swamped by sampling. So we report the coverage and the SFT reversal-timing differential — both independent of calibration and fill mode — as the campaign's robust results, and flag the per-rotation absolute g₁₀ as provisional.

The reframing

PHI's polar advantage is largest exactly when the SolO–Earth separation is largest — so the two vantages are complementary in time, not co-registered in space.

Both are driven by the same orbit: Solar Orbiter reaches high heliolatitude precisely when it is far around the Sun from Earth. When a merge is well-posed (small separation) PHI has little polar advantage; when its advantage is decisive (April, north cap, 80–172° apart) a merge is meaningless. The value of the polar view is therefore as an independent polar constraint — a boundary condition for the SFT model — at the moments Earth cannot see a given pole, not as a pixel-level merge partner.
